We answer your most asked questions

On the other subpages such as Tips and Tricks, Routes and Checklist we have listed a long list of necessities as well as tips and ways to make your journey easier and more comfortable. But sometimes it can be hard to know where to start, and you can even be left with more questions than you started out with.

This FAQ page is dedicated to answer the most common questions about hiking in Machu Picchu, ensuring that you are well-prepared and well-informed for the hiking trip. It covers essential topics such as safety measures including bringing children on the hike, how to stay safe, how much money to bring, how to avoid dehydration etc. In addition to this you also get all sorts of other relevant information about assessing your fitness level, finding the right boots and much more!



Frequently asked questions

How can I stay safe while hiking? ↓

To stay safe while hiking, it’s essential to inform someone about your plans, be aware of your surroundings, stay on marked trails, pack necessary safety equipment, and follow weather forecasts.


How do I choose the right hiking boots? ↓

Choosing the right hiking boots involves considering factors such as terrain, ankle support, comfort, and durability. It’s recommended to try on different boots and ensure a proper fit before making a decision.


How can I prevent blisters while hiking? ↓

To prevent blisters, it’s essential to wear well-fitting and moisture-wicking socks, break in your hiking boots beforehand, use blister prevention products, and stop to address any hotspots before they become blisters.


How do I handle altitude sickness? ↓

Ascend gradually. Avoid traveling from a low elevation to an elevation higher than 2,750 meters above sea level in one day. If possible, spend a few days at 2500–2750 meters before traveling to a higher elevation. This gives your body time to adjust to the lower oxygen levels.

Medicines are available to prevent acute mountain sickness and shorten the time it takes to get used to high elevations. Talk to your doctor about which is best for you given your medical history and trip plans.


What are the signs of dehydration, and how can I prevent it while hiking? ↓

Signs of dehydration include thirst, dry mouth, dizziness, fatigue, and decreased urine output. Prevent dehydration by drinking plenty of water before, during, and after your hike. Carry a sufficient amount of water and hydrate regularly. If you come across the mountain steams, you can fill up your waterbottle, but REMEMBER to use purification tablets, or boil the water


How do I choose the right hiking trail for my fitness level? ↓

Consider your fitness level, experience, and the difficulty rating of the trail. Start with trails that match your current fitness level and gradually increase the difficulty as you build strength and endurance.


How much time can I spend at the Machu Picchu sanctuary? ↓

There are three time slots in which patrons can enter Machu Picchu for a maximum of four hours and must follow one of three predetermined routes. Admission is not allowed after 4pm. Additionally, all visitors must always be accompanied by a guide. Also be aware that it is not allowed to bring food or other drinks than water, into the Machu Picchu santuary.


What can’t be brought into the Machu Picchu sanctuary ↓

The following items are prohibited in Machu Picchu: drones, selfie sticks, tripods for cameras, walking sticks without rubber tips, backpacks that exceed 40cm x 35cm x 20cm, aerosol spray, sharp objects, banners or posters.

As of December 2018, single-use plastic bottles and any other single-use plastics (bags, cups, straws, etc) are prohibited in the sanctuary, on the Inca Trail and all other protected natural areas in Peru.


Can I do the some of the routes with kids? ↓

Yes, for some of the less difficult ones it is definitely possible. We recommend they’re at least 8 years old and in good physical condition. We don't recommend you travel with kids on the trail if they aren't accustomed to regular hiking and lost of physical exertion.


Do I need to bring extra money? ↓

Yes, this is very important. We recommend that you bring 300 to 400 Peruvian soles. It is possible that you may not need any of this money, but just in case of an emergency you should make sure to have plenty of cash.

If you are struggling on the route, you can use the money to purchase alternative forms of transportation such as: car, horse, and train. On some routes, there are a couple of extra activities along the way including hot springs.
