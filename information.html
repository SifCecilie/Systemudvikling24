Machu Picchu

Machu Picchu, known as the “Lost City of the Incas,” is one of the most extraordinary archaeological sites in the world. Perched high in the Peruvian Andes, this UNESCO World Heritage site was constructed in the 15th century at the height of the Inca Empire. It is renowned for its impressive stonework, terraces, temples, and stunning location. This iconic site offers visitors a unique glimpse into the Inca civilization and attracts adventurers from around the globe.

The Inca Empire

The Inca Empire built an extensive network of roads known as the Qhapaq Ñan, which connected their vast territory. The Inca Trail to Machu Picchu is part of this network and was originally used for religious pilgrimages, communication, and military purposes. These roads allowed the Incas to efficiently manage their empire and transport goods, messages, and armies across challenging terrains. The trail leading to Machu Picchu was likely used by Incan nobility and priests, serving as a sacred path to the citadel, which was an important religious and ceremonial site. Today, hiking the Inca Trail offers a glimpse into the engineering skills and spiritual traditions of the Inca civilization.

For those wanting to reach Machu Picchu by foot, the most famous trail is the Inca Trail, which is the only route that brings you directly to the ancient ruins on foot. It’s an iconic trek that typically takes 4 to 5 days to complete, covering 42 kilometers. If you’re looking for the ultimate experience to immerse yourself fully in the history and landscape, the Inca Trail is an unforgettable journey.

Weather and customs

important. The best seasons to visit are during Peru’s dry months, from April to October, with May and September being particularly popular for their mild weather and smaller crowds. Be mindful that the Inca Trail requires permits, which are limited and can sell out months in advance. For a successful trip, booking early with a reliable guide company is essential.

The region around Machu Picchu and Cusco is rich in traditions that reflect deep Incan roots and Spanish influences. Locals hold a profound respect for Pachamama (Mother Earth), often expressed through ceremonies like Despacho offerings. Traditional clothing, marked by vibrant colors and intricate designs, plays a key cultural role, as does the sacred coca leaf, used in rituals and daily life to combat altitude effects. Hospitality is highly valued, with respectful greetings and acknowledgment of elders. Festivals such as Inti Raymi celebrate the area's heritage with dances and rituals that honor spiritual beliefs, including the worship of Apus (mountain spirits). Visitors are encouraged to approach these customs with curiosity and respect, learn basic phrases, and participate thoughtfully in local traditions.

Skill Levels

In addition to the Inca trail, there are other incredible trekking options that lead to Machu Picchu, such as:

Salkantay Trek: This alternative route takes 5 to 7 days and leads hikers past the towering, snow-capped Salkantay Mountain. Unlike the Inca Trail, this trek doesn’t require permits, so it’s ideal for last-minute travelers or those seeking a quieter experience.

Lares Trek: The Lares Trek combines scenic landscapes with a cultural experience, offering hikers the chance to visit small Andean villages and interact with local communities. Although it doesn’t lead directly to Machu Picchu, many trekkers take a train to the site at the end of the hike. This trek is generally less challenging than the Inca or Salkantay trails, making it suitable for beginners or those interested in a cultural adventure.
